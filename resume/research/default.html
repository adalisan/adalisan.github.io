Academic Non-academic
I am Sancar Adali. I have a PhD in Applied Mathematics and Statistics from Johns Hopkins University.
My research interests are Machine Learning and Statistical Pattern Recognition, specifically dimensionality reduction, manifold learning and data fusion from disparate data sources, in addition to applications of dissimilarity representation in pattern recognition analysis of graph data and graph inference problems. Please see my CV for my research and teaching experience.
Here is my resume and link to the pdf.

My thesis advisor was Professor Carey E. Priebe .

My dissertation is on using data from disparate sources in dissimilarity representation to do joint statistical inference. Here is a good introduction by Zhiliang Ma, a previous Ph.D student of my advisor.

This paper by Zhiliang Ma lays the foundation for my dissertation work.

Data Fusion from Disparate Dissimilarities and Joint Optimization of Fidelity and Commensurability

My thesis project. How to use disparate data with very different representations for inference purposes

  2009-2014

My PhD thesis was on joint embedding of disparate dissimilarities for solving various inference tasks such as transfer learning, match detection and graph matching. I have dealt with various issues such as robustness to noise and compared with alternative approaches such as Canonical Correlational Analysis. Github repo

Prediction of Heart Arrythmias

Using medical study data with multiple modalities to predict risk of heart arrythmias for the at-risk population who have been implanted with implantable cardioverter defibrillators.

  2007-2009

I was a researcher in the machine learning group for the Cardiovascular Research Grid project. We worked with the cardiologists who are custodians of a dataset involving patients with ICD implants. Our objective was to come up with robust, interpretable predictors that predicted either high risk of ICD utilization , or sudden cardiac death. An important challenge was to create predictors that both utilized both survival data and did not become biased due to not-missing-at-random data.
